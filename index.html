About Me

Welcome! I'm Sean, a junior specialist in neuroscience and biotech enthusiast with a passion for understanding the brain and identfying behavior-associated biomarkers in EEG.

In my current line of work involving several mouse models of neurodevelopmental disorders. I'm heavily involved in experimental design, behavioral experiments, craniotomy and brain surgery procedures, in vivo/in vitro neurophysiology classification, data processing, and manuscript preparation, though I'm mostly known by my lab coworkers as the "computer wizard guy" ¯\_(ツ)_/¯.

Outside of work, it's easiest to find me at the mountains hiking/rock climbing, or at the beach surfing/fortifying my sand castle. Unless I'm tired, in which case I'm usually at my computer.

Multitaper Spectrogram Implementation

Date: June 2025 - August 2025
Role: Sole Developer
Neuroscience Python Signal Processing EEG Analysis

Developed a user friendly, memory-efficient pipeline for analyzing multi-day EEG recordings in mouse models using spectral analysis. Implemented temporal chunking to process recordings that exceeded available memory, enabling automated batch processing of complete experimental datasets.

Background & Motivation

Long-duration EEG recordings from telemetry-implanted mice are essential for tracking epileptic progression and sleep-stage classification in the Silverman Lab. I implemented a wrapper for the Prerau lab's multitaper implementation (prerau.bwh.harvard.edu), but I ran into difficulties processing larger 72 hour recordings due to memory constraints on my system, limiting analysis to shorter EEG recordings we collected in other behavioral experiments.

The Prerau lab's multitaper implementation offered superior spectral resolution for identifying subtle oscillatory changes, but needed modification to handle large-scale datasets.

Approach & Methods

Extended the Prerau lab's multitaper spectrogram engine with three key optimizations:

  • Chunked processing architecture: Processes recordings in 1-hour temporal segments using lazy file loading (metadata only), enabling analysis of arbitrarily long recordings within memory constraints.
  • Dual-mode power normalization: Added relative power conversion (percentage of total) alongside absolute power, enabling consistent comparisons across recording sessions despite amplification differences.
  • Automated batch processing: Recursive file discovery, error recovery, and reporting .txts for processing entire experimental cohorts unattended.

Results & Findings

The pipeline successfully processes multi-day recordings that previously failed, primarily utilizing aggressive garbage collection after binned chunks to maintain manageable memory usage. Automated batch processing handles complete experimental cohorts with detailed error logs for quality control. The enhanced spectral resolution revealed subtle frequency dynamics during sleep stage events that were obscured in traditional EEG waveform visualizers, allowing for cursory identification of waveform regions for downstream spiking analysis and PSD.

Impact & Conclusions

This work removed barriers to large-scale EEG analysis in our lab, enabling routine examination of multi-day recordings. The enhanced spectral detail provides quantifiable metrics for assessing therapeutic efficacy in our animal models. Most importantly, though, is the user-friendly GUI that allows less technical peers to populate the customizable features of Prerau's multitaper spectrograms, allowing everyone the opportunity to look at the EEG data we've collected.

Homemade EEG and BCI Personal Project

Date: August 2025 - Present
Role: Personal Project Lead :)
BCI Machine Learning Signal Processing Python

I am currently in the early-mid stages of a passion project where I'm building a real-time brain-computer interface to detect motor imagery (imagined muscle clenching) from single-channel EEG recordings on myself to translate my EEG signals into keyboard commands in real time.

Background & Motivation

Hobbyist BCI toolkits can be costly, especially for someone with my current budget, limiting my accessibility for personal education and experimentation. I found that there was some online documentation (found here) for RC circuit enthusiasts that illustrated the necessary components needed to make your own homemade EEG setup off of a breadboard, resistors/capacitors, and some select ICs, running me about $40 total in raw supply costs. I particularly liked this approach as it additionally included instructions for several critical analogue noise filtering stages at 60Hz notch filtering, 31Hz LFP, 1HZ HPF while also utilizing the computer's mic-in port and soundcard as a final-stage ADC. The ultimate goal was to utilize this as a recording data acquisition platform to allow me to self-experiment functional motor imagery detection tasks, akin to the sensory evoked potentials experiments we conduct in the Silverman Lab!


Most of all, I've had a long-standing interest in prosthetic devices and body/brain/machine interfaces, and I thought it'd be fulfilling to begin personal work in this domain as a passion project.

Technical Implementation

Utilizing the medical grade instrumentation amplifier AD620 as per the enthusiast guide, I take in the two inputs from 2 electrodes and compute the difference between them, amplified by a controllable gain multiplier dependent on the resistence between pins 1 and 8. The next 6 main stages are comrpised of the 60Hz notch filter (to reduce electrical noise), a 7Hz high pass filter, a 31Hz low pass filter, a 1Hz high pass filter, a necessary gain stage to improve signal to noise ratio, and one last 60Hz notch filter. It's not described in the guide, but I did some further digging on how this phenomenom arose, and it was really interesting. Since capacitors are capable of storing a charge (the degree of which is dependent on it's specifications), wiring either end of the capacitor in the same circuit allows for interesting oscilitory activity of the signal, which, when the correct parameters are selected, allows you to selectively wash out (or amplify!) specific frequencies of interest. From there, in order to standardize electrode placement during the experiment, I built my own 1 channel 2 electrode EEG cap with some hot glue, electric tape, and a ski mask :)

Hardware aside, I then developed four simple Python modules to make a complete BCI pipeline, namely a validation real-time visualization (of the activity and time-domain + FFT) using circular buffering of the mic in EEG data to verify circuit quality. I produced my own psychology behavioral task using PsychoPy (on an unfortunately older Python version) to present pseudo-randomized visual cues to myself (trials such as "CLENCH" vs "REST") with synchronized 3-second EEG recordings, collecting 80 trials per session with variable inter-trial intervals.

Then, I preprocess my collected data: I downsample from 48 kHz (mic in sampling rate) to 250 Hz, apply fourth-order Butterworth bandpass (8-30 Hz) to isolate alpha through beta frequencies, extract log-transformed bandpower features using Welch's method, and then I'm able to train a logistic regression ML model with 5-fold stratified cross-validation. Lastly, it's just a matter of screening my live-data feed from my mic continuously at 1 second EEG windows at 4 before triggering my selected keyboard output.



Results & Performance

Successfully demonstrated the complete pipeline from signal acquisition through real-time actuation. The modular architecture enables independent optimization of hardware (circuit design), software (feature engineering), and user factors (motor imagery technique). Real-time classification achieves ~4 Hz update rate with 0.75-second total latency including debouncing—suitable for discrete control tasks. Cross-validation framework is implemented to evaluate classifier generalization, with accuracy dependent on signal-to-noise ratio from the DIY circuit. The PsychoPy interface provides millisecond-precision stimulus timing for building clean labeled datasets.

Learnings & Future Work

The primary challenge has been achieving consistent signal quality from DIY hardware—EEG amplitudes are only 10-100 µV, requiring high gain while minimizing 60 Hz line noise and motion artifacts. This provided hands-on experience with differential amplification, electrode impedance, and grounding strategies. Feature engineering proved critical: while simple bandpower works as a baseline, incorporating spatial information (multi-channel), temporal dynamics, or advanced spectral features could improve robustness.

The project highlighted BCI's interdisciplinary nature—spanning neuroscience (event-related desynchronization), signal processing (filtering, spectral estimation), machine learning (classification, validation), and experimental design (randomization, timing control). Future extensions include multi-class classification (left/right hand discrimination), continuous control (variable speed output), adaptive calibration for baseline drift, and hybrid paradigms combining motor imagery with P300 or SSVEP.

To Do

Continuing signal acquisition optimization with circuit redesign for improved common-mode rejection. Beginning systematic behavioral data collection across multiple sessions to build larger training datasets for more robust cross-session generalization. Exploring multi-channel acquisition (C3/C4 electrodes) to leverage spatial features and implement lateralization decoding.
